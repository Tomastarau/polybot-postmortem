## The verdict

Across 752 paper bets the bot made 1.12 dollars. The win rate is 88.3 %, and it
means nothing.

Here is why. On these markets a bet costs its price and always pays 1.00 dollar
if it wins. Paying 0.88 to win 1.00 means risking 0.88 to pocket 0.12. To break
even you therefore have to win **88 times out of 100**. The win rate is the
posted price, restated as a percentage. Winning 88 % of the time while paying
0.88 is doing exactly nothing.

The chart below overlays the two. In every price band the blue bar (what the bot
achieved) lands on the red dot (what it needed to achieve). Only one place opens a
gap: bets bought between 0.70 and 0.85, at +4.4 % return over 175 bets. It is the
only lead these three months produced, and it stays fragile: on the training half
it returns just +1.45 %, which is indistinguishable from chance.

That left the final arithmetic, and there is nothing sophisticated about it. The
server cost 12 dollars a month. The best estimate of gains, assuming the lead
above is real, was around 9 dollars a month. Available capital was 36 dollars.

An edge is expressed as a percentage; a server is paid in euros. While capital
stays small, any fixed cost dominates the equation. No amount of strategy
refinement fixes a denominator of 36 dollars.

So I stopped the bot, deleted the infrastructure, and published the data. This
site is what remains: 1.27 million decisions, reduced to 5.3 megabytes, and a
negative but solid conclusion.
